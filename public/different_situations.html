Changing magnetic field
### Changing current inside the wire in my opinion when current changes then its another different equation of force between the particles. its a bit interesting but not that interesting for me to dive in. so maybe i will check it later ### Moving wire when wire moves its same as the protons + electrons get some speed, so we can simply measure whats the force that is being made on the electrons in the other wire, and then do (force/q)*distance to get the voltage. calculating that way gives almost exactly the same results as the Faraday's Law. shortly saying, when looking on the force on the electrons the equation looks like this: $$2 \cdot Top\ Part = \begin{aligned} {{q_1}_\+ + {q_2}_\- \rightarrow} + 2(\vec{v_1}_\+ - \vec{v_2}_\-)^2 - 3(\vec{v_1}_\+ \cdot \hat{r} - \vec{v_2}_\- \cdot \hat{r})^2 \\\\ {{q_1}_\- + {q_2}_\- \rightarrow} - 2(\vec{v_1}_\- - \vec{v_2}_\-)^2 + 3(\vec{v_1}_\- \cdot \hat{r} - \vec{v_2}_\- \cdot \hat{r})^2 \end{aligned}$$ and then just checking the force in current direction: \\(\vec{F} \parallel \hat{I_2}\\) because the only movement that matters is in the current direction. you can see the voltage [there](#calc_two_circles), when the toggle is unchecked its the Faraday's Law, when its checked then it uses my new equation to calculate the voltage. what about the force on protons? why its not (force on electrons - force on protons) = voltage ? well i think it also should matter, but because the proton is so heavy relative to the electron, even if he will have some movement its basically nothing relative to the electron movement. so i just ignore the force on the protons when calculating the voltage. ### faradays paradox my equation seems to work also when the faradays changing magnetic field law didnt worked. you can check it [here](comparing_equations.html#faradays_paradox) #
Extras
1. if you look at the short equation: $$\vec{F} = \frac{Constant \cdot |I_1| \cdot |I_2| \cdot (2 \cdot \hat{I_1} \cdot \hat{I_2} - 3(\hat{I_1} \cdot \hat{r}) \cdot (\hat{I_2} \cdot \hat{r}))}{\vec{r}\ ^2}$$ you can see that the value of I is linear inside the equation. so even if inside the wire the resistence changes, or the pressure changes, or the speed changes, it doesnt matters. because as long as q*v=I, the equation will still have the same result even if v changes (because I is stable). 2. My equation also tells that when electron rotates around the proton then its a pulling force, and when the electron falls into the proton then its a pushing force. which explain why the electron cant just fall into the atom core.
